XP Pen Artist Display 12 Review: Reliable Mid-Range Tablet With Minor Trade-Offs

Electronics Drawing Tools Tablet Digital Art Illustration Budget Tech

By 3lectricTouch | Published March 22, 2026

The XP Pen Artist Display 12 is the second drawing tablet I have ever owned, and one I have used for many years. I purchased this tablet because the first one I owned didn't have a screen and was a cheaper, beginner tablet, and the wiring was a bit faulty, so I thought it was time for an upgrade. But is the XP Pen Artist Display actually worth it, or should a different tablet be purchased instead?

For approximately $200, I purchased the model with an 11.6-inch diagonal screen, and it came with six customizable shortcut keys and a touch bar. These features are great, since drawing tablets in a similar price range are not touchscreens. Having shortcut keys provides a more convenient, personalized drawing experience, and the touch bar can be used to zoom in and out of a canvas. Along with the tablet, the box included the necessary cables, a drawing glove, a cleaning cloth, a pen holder, a battery-free pen, replacement nibs, and adapters, which are great additions to the product.

The pen I had for my previous drawing tablet had to be either connected via a cable or charged beforehand, so a battery-free one is far more convenient. Additionally, the pen has a digital eraser on the back, a shortcut button equivalent to a right-mouse click, and over 8,000 levels of pressure sensitivity. Although many replacement nibs comes with the tablet, I have yet to use any of them.

The back of the tablet sits at a slightly upward angle, which made my personal drawing experience more comfortable. The display is relatively accurate and offers high image quality, and you can even adjust the brightness on your tablet. Not to mention, the screen also has an anti-glare coating on it, which is another positive. Considering I acquired the tablet about five years ago and I still use it on occasion, the XP Pen Artist Display 12 is a relatively reliable tablet.

However, the product is not perfect. To use the tablet, you must connect it to your computer with the provided cable, which requires three ports: two USB, one HDMI, and one port on the tablet. The wires can be long and annoying since I have to use the provided extension cord, and setting it up at first can be confusing, which also makes the drawing a bit inconvenient, especially if I need to regularly take my laptop with me to school or work. I found that the wires do not seem to hold up well over time, so I recently had to replace them after my tablet screen started flashing a series of rainbow colors, costing me an additional twenty dollars. The wires lasted me about three to four years. But within the last two years or so, the tablet did not see consistent use. The life of the wires might be shorter or longer depending on how often you are moving the set up around or plugging and unplugging wires.

There is also a difference in color display between my laptop and tablet, though I have yet to investigate it because the difference is minor. Another issue to note is that if your laptop screen is not a standard size (mine is taller than normal), that will not translate well to the tablet. The tablet will attempt to fit the exact dimensions of your laptop screen onto its own, potentially making the display smaller, which made me zoom in much more often when I got my new laptop.

Overall, the XP Pen Artist 12 is still a reliable tablet that will last a few years, even if the cables may need to be replaced occasionally. The issues, such as wire longevity, color display, and port usage, are minor, and you are getting a great deal for the price point. Some people classify the tablet as entry-level, though I believe it is more of an intermediate-level tablet, since it would be better for those who are just getting started with digital art and are not sure if they like it, to start with a cheaper tablet.

Summary

Rating:

For $200, the XP Pen Artist 12 is a reliable drawing tablet that will last you for years until your next upgrade. It is a great tablet for beginner and intermediate digital artists, though it has minor shortcomings.

Pros

  • Relatively affordable
  • High-quality built-in display screen
  • Customizable shortcut keys and touch bar
  • Battery-free pen with replacement nib
  • High-pressure sensitivity levels
  • Comes with many accessories
  • Comfortable angled design
  • Good durability

Cons

  • Wired connection
  • Requires two USB ports and one HMDI port
  • Not very portable (due to wires)
  • Cable durability issues
  • Initial setup confusion
  • Minor color mismatch
  • Screen scaling issues (for non-standard laptop displays)

Anatomy for Artists: Right Idea, Mixed Execution

Anatomy Study Figure Drawing Skill Building Art Fundamentals Learning Resources Practice Workbook

By i_hate_hands | Published March 25, 2026

Since childhood, I have enjoyed drawing greatly, and I have been praised for my artistic abilities. But as I grew older, my eye for picking out what is “good art” grew a lot faster than my skills. I gradually stopped drawing because I wasn’t able to properly execute the ideas in my head, and I was lacking in some areas, especially anatomy. So when I came across this book, Anatomy for Artists: Complete With Draw-In Pages to Refine Your Skills by Barrington Barber, at Barnes & Noble for ten dollars, I thought I had found the answer to my problems.

At first glance, the book seems great. It is broken down into sections of the human body and is very detailed, with diagrams of skeletons and muscles. Differences in male and female anatomy are discussed, and there is even a glossary of anatomical terminology. As advertised in the book’s title, for each section, there are accompanying pages where you can practice drawing the muscles and bones. For your convenience, the general outline is provided as well. These features and the level of detail would be great for some artists learning anatomy, but not for all.

As a long-time artist, I believe that the book is overkill for many artists. As mentioned, the book explains the muscles and bones, and some people may be interested in what each tiny part is called. But if the book is advertised as “anatomy for artists”, knowing anatomy to that extent, in my opinion, is unnecessary. There isn’t any harm in drawing out the specific bones and muscles the first time you are introduced to a certain part of the body, but that shouldn’t be the focus of learning anatomy. I believe the book would be much better if, in addition to the existing practice pages, there were a section that explains breaking those muscles into simpler forms and emphasizing that step instead. That way, artists can reduce noise and focus on what matters: understanding the shapes of muscles and bones enough to form a mental image and create different poses for figure drawings and illustrations. Trying to copy each individual bone and muscle strand over and over again is tedious and, frankly, discouraging over time, because it will likely yield very slow results. Not to mention, for most people, learning anatomy for purposes such as posing and character illustrations, the bones, muscles, and organs that a person spent so much time learning now to draw often won’t be seen, as they will either be covered by skin or clothes.

Additionally, there are many free resources, such as YouTube and TikTok, that offer tutorials on breaking down the human body, and anatomical diagrams can be found there as well. If someone is really interested in understanding each component of the human body, one can refer to anatomy videos geared towards the medical field instead. It might be convenient for some to draw directly on the pages provided in the book, but the features themselves can be found elsewhere online, and the cost of the book can be better spent on materials or more specialized resources

The book Anatomy for Artists: Complete With Draw-In Pages to Refine Your Skills provides decent value for the price, but it is hardly life-changing, since it doesn’t offer anything unique. The book may be a better fit for those learning anatomy for the first time, those who want to draw anatomy with great detail, or those who benefit from a very structured learning approach, but far less effective for intermediate artists. With some changes, the book will be more helpful to a larger group of artists.

Summary

Rating:

While Anatomy for Artists: Complete With Draw-In Pages to Refine Your Skills is very detailed and organized, it emphasizes memorizing anatomy too heavily rather than simplification for flexibility, and may prove less useful in the long run for intermediate artists or those seeking efficient improvement.

Pros

  • Affordable
  • Explains anatomy in depth
  • Organized by body sections
  • Includes pages with a general outline to practice drawing anatomy
  • Contains a glossary of anatomical terms
  • Good for artists new to anatomy

Cons

  • Too much emphasis on the details
  • Focuses on memorization instead of simplification
  • Many free sources online offer similar value
  • Limited usefulness for intermediate artists
